Understanding Hyponatremia

Definition

Hyponatremia is defined as a serum [Na+] <135 mmol/L and can be acute or chronic. The treatment of hyponatremia is guided by severity, chronicity, and etiology – so determination of all of these factors is paramount.

  • Acute: <48 hours since development of hyponatremia.
  • Chronic: >48 hours since development of hyponatremia.

Note: hyponatremia should be considered chronic whenever the duration is unknown.

Severity Levels:

  • Mild: serum [Na+] 130-134 mmol/L.
  • Moderate: serum [Na+] 120-129 mmol/L.
  • Severe: serum [Na+] <120 mmol/L.

Hyponatremia is the most common electrolyte abnormality in hospitalized patients (estimated to occur in 10-30% of all hospitalized patients). Recognition and safe treatment are extremely important. Hyponatremia and its mismanagement can cause serious, sudden, and irreversible neurological problems and is associated with increased mortality.

Etiology

Hyponatremia represents an excess of free water and should be considered a disorder of water balance.

Excess free water can result from:

  • High intake (e.g. polydipsia).
  • Impaired free water excretion.
  • Low GFR or diminished diluting capacity of the kidney (e.g. dialysis patients).
  • High ADH (e.g. heart failure, SIADH).
  • Inadequate solute intake (e.g. tea and toast diet).
  • A combination of high intake & low excretion.

Evaluation

Helpful tips:
  • Remember that free water balance (urine osmolality) is regulated by ADH.
  • Sodium excretion (urine sodium) is regulated by aldosterone.
  • If a patient has end-stage kidney disease, the cause of hyponatremia is excess free water intake in the setting of impaired kidney water excretion, and is not mediated by ADH.

Step 1: Differentiate true hyponatremia from pseudohyponatremia.

A normal or elevated effective serum osmolality (≥280 mOsm/kg) suggests pseudohyponatremia or hypertonic hyponatremia.

Hypertonic states, like hyperglycemia or mannitol use, can cause hyponatremia by drawing water extracellularly and lowering serum sodium concentration. This is technically a true (not pseudo) hyponatremia. Serum osmolality will be high; sodium should normalize with correction of hypertonic state (insulin for hyperglycemia, excretion of mannitol, etc.).

Correction formula for hyperglycemia: corrected Na = measured Na + [(serum glucose – 100)/100] * 1.6 (Note: factor may vary, 2.4 is also commonly used).

Hyperlipidemia or increased protein can also cause a lab error that results in a falsely low sodium result (pseudohyponatremia). Blood gas analyzers are typically not subject to this error.

Step 2: Is ADH high or low? Compare serum osmolality to urine osmolality (for Hypotonic Hyponatremia).

If Serum Osm > Urine Osm (or Urine Osm <100 mOsm/kg), then ADH is appropriately suppressed (kidneys are maximizing water excretion, i.e. maximally dilute urine). The problem is excess water intake or inadequate solute intake.

  • Check for iatrogenesis: are there high rates of hypotonic solution infusing?
  • Look at what medications are mixed in (e.g. IV bactrim in 500 ml D5W 4x/day = 2L of free water).
  • Check volume of free water flushes in patients getting tube feeds or other hypotonic oral intake.
  • Consider primary polydipsia (formerly known as “psychogenic polydipsia”).
  • Consider causes of inadequate solute intake: include "tea and toast" diet and "beer potomania" (high alcohol intake relative to solute intake).
    • Because urine osm cannot be 0, there is always some obligate solute excreted with urine. Even if maximally dilute at 50 mOsm/L, if a person only eats 100 mOsm and drinks 3L of water in a day, they can only eliminate 100/50 = 2L of that water and 1L is retained. This dilutes the serum sodium.
    • Carbohydrates and fat metabolize to water and CO2, so do not count as solute. Protein and electrolytes (e.g., sodium) are solutes. Hence, diets like toast and beer contain insufficient solute.

If Serum Osm < Urine Osm (or Urine Osm >100-150 mOsm/kg), then ADH effect is present (inappropriately high for the serum osmolality, or appropriately high due to non-osmotic stimuli). Proceed to Step 3.

Step 3: If ADH effect is present, what is the volume status?

Tips:
  • If ADH is activated, urine osmolality is usually >100 mOsm/kg.
  • Urine sodium can help determine RAAS activation, which can narrow your differential in cases where volume status is not clear. A low urine sodium (<20-30 mEq/L) suggests RAAS activation (seen in hyper- or hypovolemia); a high urine sodium (>40 mEq/L) may suggest SIADH (in the absence of diuretics or salt wasting).
Hypervolemic:

ADH is released in response to low effective arterial blood volume (EABV) due to third spacing or poor forward flow.

  • Ddx: CHF, nephrotic syndrome, liver failure (cirrhosis).
  • Urine Na will typically be low (<20-30 mEq/L), since the RAAS system is also activated in response to low EABV (unless on diuretics).
Hypovolemic:

ADH is being released in response to low EABV due to fluid loss.

  • Extrarenal losses (e.g., GI loss, sweat, burns): Urine Na will be low (<20-30 mEq/L), since aldosterone is also active.
  • Renal salt wasting: Urine Na will be high (>40 mEq/L). Causes include intrinsic salt-wasting nephropathy, primary adrenal insufficiency, cerebral salt wasting (CSW), and thiazide diuretic use.
Euvolemic:
  • Hypothyroidism: check TSH (usually severe hypothyroidism).
  • Glucocorticoid deficiency (Secondary Adrenal Insufficiency): check AM cortisol or perform ACTH stimulation test.
  • SIADH: inappropriate release of ADH independent of EABV/osmolality or release of ADH at a lower osm level (“reset osmostat”). Urine Na will typically be high (>40 mEq/L), since aldosterone is suppressed by normal/high effective volume.
    • Etiology: pain/nausea, pulmonary disease (pneumonia, TB), CNS disease (stroke, hemorrhage, infection), malignancy (esp. small cell lung cancer), drugs (SSRIs, MDMA/ecstasy, opioids, chemotherapy [e.g. cyclophosphamide, vincristine]).
    • SIADH is a diagnosis of exclusion with the following features:
      • Clinical euvolemia.
      • Normal thyroid and adrenal function, no recent diuretic use.
      • Lab findings:
        • Urine osmolality >100-150 mOsm/kg (often > Serum osmolality).
        • Serum osmolality <275 mOsm/kg.
        • Urine Na >20-40 mmol/L with normal dietary salt intake.
      • Corroborating features (sometimes but not always present):
        • Serum uric acid <4mg/dl.
        • BUN <10 mg/dL.
        • FENa >1-2% or FEUrea >45-55%.
        • Refractory or worsening hyponatremia after isotonic fluid infusion.

Management

Evaluate and treat severe symptoms emergently in all patients.

  • Check for signs of cerebral edema: visual changes, neurologic deficits, encephalopathy, and seizures. The presence of these signs suggests cerebral edema and necessitates rapid treatment.
  • To acutely relieve cerebral edema, sodium can be raised using 3% hypertonic saline (100 mL bolus given over 10 minutes, may be repeated up to 2 times).
  • Goal is resolution of severe symptoms, typically with [Na+] rise of 4-6 mEq/L.
  • Usual rate limit of correction still applies, i.e. if the goal is 6 mEq/L/day, and acute treatment for severe symptoms has already increased [Na+] by 6 mEq/L, then that level of sodium should be maintained for the following 24 hours.

Calculate desired rate of correction.

  • The brain adapts to hypotonic hyponatremia with intracellular osmolar shifts over approximately 48 hours.
  • For acute hyponatremia (<48 hours), rapid correction is appropriate and generally safe.
  • Chronic hyponatremia (>48 hours or unknown duration) requires slower correction (no more than 6-8 mEq/L/day) to minimize the risk of osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS). If the duration of hyponatremia is unknown, always assume it is chronic and use slower correction goals.
  • Risk factors for ODS: lower sodium (<105 mEq/L), hypokalemia, malnutrition, alcohol use disorder, liver disease.

Select treatment approach based on volume status, severity, and etiology.

  • Hypovolemia: The cornerstone of treatment is volume expansion with isotonic crystalloid (0.9% NaCl) to restore EABV.
    • Caution: Once volume status is corrected, ADH stimulus is removed, and a brisk aquaresis (water diuresis) may ensue, causing overcorrection. Strict urine output monitoring and frequent sodium checks are critical. The earliest sign of impending overcorrection is often a rapid increase in urine output and/or a decrease in urine osmolality. For patients at high risk of ODS, consider preemptive strategies like DDAVP (see below) - nephrology consultation recommended.
  • SIADH:
    • Treat underlying cause/withdraw causative agents if possible.
    • Start with fluid restriction 1-1.5 L/day. Restriction alone is often inadequate, especially if Uosm is very high. Do not restrict beyond 1L without clear benefit, as it can cause significant discomfort.
    • Use urine electrolytes (UNa + UK) to guide therapy:
      • Based on the concept of electrolyte free water clearance (EFWC). Compare UNa + UK to plasma Na.
      • UNa + UK < serum Na: Positive free water clearance. Kidneys are excreting some free water. Fluid restriction is likely to be effective.
      • UNa + UK > serum Na: Negative free water clearance. All free water is reabsorbed; urination concentrates the body further. Fluid restriction alone is unlikely to work. Osmole supplementation (e.g., salt tabs, urea, hypertonic saline) is needed.
      • Any IV fluids with Na content less than UNa + UK will result in worsening hyponatremia (desalination). If UNa + UK > 154 (Na content of NS), NS infusion will worsen hyponatremia.
    • Additional therapies for SIADH:
      • Hypertonic saline (3%): Effective (513 mEq/L), necessary when UNa + UK is very high or for severe/symptomatic hyponatremia. Requires careful monitoring and good venous access (central line often preferred for sustained infusions). Not a long-term therapy.
      • Loop diuretics: Can help excrete free water by impairing medullary concentration, but efficacy is variable. May be useful as an adjunct to NaCl tabs to manage volume if needed.
      • NaCl tabs (1g NaCl tab = 17 mEq Na+): Requires high pill burden (e.g., 2-3g TID). Can stimulate thirst and cause GI upset.
      • Oral urea (powder or tabs): 15g = 250 mOsm. Acts as an osmotic diuretic to eliminate free water. Efficient osmole load, less likely to cause volume overload, good safety margin.
  • Hypervolemia:
    • Free water restriction (1-1.5 L/day).
    • Loop diuretics to optimize volume status and promote water/sodium excretion.
    • Hypertonic saline and salt tabs generally NOT recommended as they will worsen volume overload.

Role of DDAVP (Desmopressin, ADH analog) for management of hyponatremia

  • DDAVP (dosed 1-2mcg IV or SC q6h-q8h) provides a constant maximal ADH effect, preventing unpredictable water excretion ("aquaresis") and thus preventing overly rapid correction of serum sodium, especially when the underlying ADH stimulus resolves. This facilitates controlled correction, often used with hypertonic saline ("DDAVP clamp").
  • Typically DDAVP is considered as a proactive measure when ODS risk is high and brisk aquaresis is expected, such as in:
    • Hypovolemic hyponatremia after fluid resuscitation.
    • Hyponatremia due to poor solute intake after solute is provided (e.g., NS infusion).
    • SIADH when the stimulus for ADH may resolve suddenly (e.g., stopping an offending drug).
  • DDAVP may also be used as a reactive measure to "put the brakes" on established overcorrection, often along with D5W infusion.
  • Do NOT use DDAVP in patients with volume overload (CHF, cirrhosis) or patients who may continue to drink water/hypotonic fluids uncontrolled (e.g., primary polydipsia), as this can worsen fluid retention and hyponatremia.

If the serum sodium has been overcorrected:

  • Administer IV D5W (5% Dextrose in Water - provides free water).
  • Consider adding DDAVP (as above) to prevent further renal water loss.
  • Can estimate D5W rate: ~3mL/kg/hour D5W might lower [Na+] by approximately 1 mEq/L/hour (individual response varies).
  • Pay attention to ongoing losses: If urine output is brisk (>150 mL/hour) and dilute (low Uosm), the patient is losing free water rapidly, driving serum [Na+] up quickly. Reactive boluses of D5W may be inadequate. Urgent nephrology consultation is advised for safe correction strategies (e.g., titrating D5W drip based on urine output, using DDAVP).

Key Points

  • Hypotonic hyponatremia is due to excess free water, which may be a result of excess water intake and/or impaired water excretion.
  • Severe symptoms of hyponatremia represent an emergency and should be treated with hypertonic saline to immediately raise [Na+] by 4-6 mEq/L to resolve symptoms.
  • The recommended correction rate is 6-8 mEq/L per 24 hours for chronic hyponatremia (or unknown duration) to prevent ODS.
  • If there is high risk for ODS or if considering DDAVP, sustained hypertonic infusion (beyond emergent use), or vaptan therapy, a nephrology consult is advised.
  • Hypovolemic, low solute, and hypervolemic hyponatremia are primarily treated by correcting the underlying cause and managing volume/solute balance. Urine output monitoring is critical as it is the earliest sign of potentially overly rapid correction.
  • SIADH is treated by addressing the underlying cause (if possible) but often requires specific therapy (fluid restriction, salt/urea supplementation) to manage water balance. Urine Na + K can be used to assess severity and guide therapy.
